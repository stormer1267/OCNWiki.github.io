Development News

This week, Cubist writes about a large project that he has been tackling.

What I was working on all week was rewriting a part of our code called Tracker. This code is ultimately responsible for generating death messages, and in order to do that, it has to keep track of all sorts of information about the events leading up to a player's death.

For example, when you kill a player by knocking them off a bridge, Tracker is the thing that remembers who hit them and gives you the kill when they hit the ground later. When you get a TNT kill, it's because Tracker remembered which TNT blocks were placed by you, which TNT entities came from those blocks, and which other TNT blocks the explosion activated. It also tracks projectiles, pet mobs (spawned from eggs), fire, lava, dispensers, anvils, and spleefing.

And it can track any combination of these things, so you can place a dispenser, that dispenses a skeleton with a flame bow, that shoots some TNT, which destroys a bridge and makes a player fall into lava, which sets them on fire and later burns them to death, and tracker will trace it all the way back to you.

The old Tracker code has been around for years now, and has become really messy from features being layered on one at a time. The code has become really hard to work with, and a major source of bugs. When code gets like this, smart developers will do something called "refactoring", which essentially means changing how the code works without changing what it does. Because we now understand exactly what the code needs to do, we can write it much better than we could back when we were feeling out new features one at a time. Software projects that are very large and/or old usually require constant refactoring just to keep them alive.

Anyway, as I write this, the new Tracker is going through some final testing, and it may even be deployed by the time you read this. While this was mostly an investment in future development, there are a few improvements that will be immediately visible to players.

Death messages are now fully localized, meaning they can be translated into other languages. Monti and the translation team are no doubt waiting to sink their teeth into this. It wasn't possible to translate the messages from the old Tracker, because they were pieced together word by word, as the cause of death was unraveled, and this process was tightly coupled to English grammar rules. The new Tracker assembles messages in a similar way, but instead of an English sentence, it generates a translation key like "death.fall.void.melee.player.weapon". The English translation for that key is "{0} was knocked out of the world by {1}'s {2}". Every possible death message has its own key and is translated independently, and it looks like there will be about 120 of them in total. There are a lot of ways to die on OCN.

Another improvement that you might notice is that a player's kills are properly tracked after they switch teams or leave the match. The old Tracker would get confused if you shot an arrow and then left your team before it hit something, or placed some TNT and left before it exploded. The new Tracker handles all that properly. That entire chain reaction I described above can happen long after you leave the server, and it will make no difference. Your name will be in the death message, with the correct team color, and your stats will be updated. This works for monuments as well. You can no longer blow up your own monument by placing TNT and then switching teams. TNT behaves the same no matter what you do after you place it.

Feature: Of Flying and its Democratization

In this article, Tywnis gives his thoughts on the use of the fly kit module in maps.

The Fly kit module has existed for a while now. It takes map design to an interesting new level, yet we see very few maps that use it. Being one of the makers, if not the only one, to have made maps using it, here are a few observations i have regarding its usage.

Several things have to be taken in account:

First of all, boundaries. It may seem a bit negative to start off with what limits the game, but that’s the most important thing: you have to define where you want players to be able to go.

Exterior boundaries-wise, I first contemplated the idea of giving a poison kit to players exiting, and heal them back were they to enter the zone again. I failed to make it work in a satisfactory and self-explanatory manner, so i moved on.

I then used an XML barrier, denying players the possibility to exit a defined zone. However, this solution results in players glitching or getting stucked into the barrier, hence it is not recommended.

The third and best solution is to use hard blocks to confine the game in a box. The block 166 will be helpful here, as it is fully transparent.

Within the “box”, limiting player movement translates more through guiding it in new and revolutionary layouts. The Y axis can now be developed much more.

Second of all, modes.

Players move fast - in these conditions regular pvp quickly becomes difficult. Because of the speed, and because flying allows for a whole new set of moves and dodgings, including controlled falls, it seems important to simplify the killing process. The Rage sub-type seems like a good answer, as it decreases the tediousness of this kind of pvp - although this doesn’t mean that we won’t see non-rage pvp fly maps in the future...

Gamemode-wise, we should also remember that not all modes include fighting. I look forward to what the community could come up with in a certain area specifically: race maps with fly enabled. This could get quite entertaining, in my opinion…

It is also worth noting that destroy-based gamemodes such as DTM or DTC might not fit particularly well with the fly module. Unless we were to add some TNT based weaponry…

And lastly, I would like to express my excitement towards this module. It is rather new, and there’s a lot left to explore. Our community has been growing up in many ways, but it’s not the end of the road just yet…
